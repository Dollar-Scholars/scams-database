Common scams to recognize

Tap each one to see what it looks like in real life.

You get a text or email that looks like it's from your bank, a delivery company, or a government office. It says there's a problem and asks you to click a link and log in. The link goes to a fake site built to steal your password. Real companies never ask you to confirm a password through a text link.

Someone offers a job with great pay for very little work, often by text or social media. Before you start, they ask you to pay for training or equipment, or to deposit a check and send part of it back. A real employer never asks you to pay them before you start working.

You get a call, text, or letter claiming to be from immigration, the IRS, or another agency. It says you owe a fee or risk losing your status unless you pay right away, often by gift card or wire transfer. Government agencies contact you by official mail first, and they never demand gift cards.

You're buying or selling something online — a car, an apartment, furniture. The other person pushes you to pay outside the platform, send a deposit before you see it in person, or sends a check for more than the price and asks you to refund the difference. Never pay for something you haven't seen in person.

A pop-up or phone call claims your computer has a virus and offers to "fix" it if you let them remotely access your device or pay for software. Once they're in, they can see your files and accounts. Hang up — real tech companies don't call you out of the blue about a virus.

Someone you met online builds a relationship with you over weeks or months, then asks for money for an emergency, travel, or an investment, even though you've never met in person. If someone you've never met in person asks you for money, that's a strong sign something is wrong.

If you suspect a scam

  1. Stop. Don't send money, gift cards, or personal information.
  2. Verify independently. Call the company or agency yourself, using a number from their official website — not the one the caller gave you.
  3. Talk to someone you trust before you act — a family member, friend, or community organization.
  4. Report it, using the resources below, so others can be warned too.

Where to report a scam

FTC File a report with the Federal Trade Commission. FBI IC3 Report internet-based fraud to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center. CFPB Learn your rights and file a complaint about a financial company. Your State AG Find your state Attorney General's consumer protection office.

This page shares general information and isn't legal advice. If you're in immediate danger or have already lost money, contact local law enforcement or a qualified attorney right away.
